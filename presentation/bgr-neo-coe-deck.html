03 · Why now

The ecosystem already exists. We plug into it — we don't invent it.

The people

Engineers, not yet job-ready

Tamil Nadu graduates engineers in volume. Very few have touched an industrial robot, and almost none have commissioned a cell. The gap is not talent — it is exposure.

The skill gap

AI and robotics, together

Factories are adding vision, predictive maintenance and AI-assisted programming. The people who can do both sides — the robot and the AI — barely exist.

The push

Government is already funding this

Central and state skilling bodies are actively standing up centres for exactly AI, robotics and advanced manufacturing. The money and the mechanism are live now.

Central · MSDE

National Centres of Excellence for Skilling

Launched targeting AI, robotics and advanced manufacturing, delivering nationally-aligned training at NSQF levels ~5–8, operationalised through National Skill Training Institutes.

State · TNSDC

The Centre of Excellence programme

Tamil Nadu's nodal skilling agency runs a CoE programme where industry partners set up high-end skilling facilities, selected by an Expression of Interest. This is our route.

State · Naan Mudhalvan

Reach into every college

The Chief Minister's flagship embeds industry courses into engineering curricula as mandatory credit, via train-the-trainer, targeting 10+ lakh students. Our future feeder.

05 · Our recommendation

Model C — a state-first Centre of Excellence via TNSDC, built so it can become our own company later.

Reason 01

It is the only model that uses our real advantage

A training-partner contract or a college course cannot put a student on a live robot cell. A Centre of Excellence can — and that is the one thing we have that others don't.

Reason 02

It is winnable, through a door that is already open

TNSDC's CoE selection runs on an Expression of Interest judged by a committee. It is a process we can enter this year, not a scheme we must lobby into existence.

Reason 03

It keeps the bigger plays open

Starting at state level does not close the national route or full ownership. It earns the track record that makes both easier to win later.

The template for later

TNASDCL

Tamil Nadu's Apex Skill Development Centre for Logistics — a Section 8 not-for-profit formed as a partnership between TNSDC, a sector skill council and industry (TVS). Precisely the structure a BGR Neo skilling company would take.

Proof it can earn

TN Semiconductor Mission CoEs

Established as for-profit special-purpose vehicles with academia and industry. A Centre of Excellence does not have to be a cost centre — there is a live precedent for it being a business.

08 · Eligibility — the raw-material spec

We test what a candidate can do, not which degree they hold.

The gate

A short entrance assessment

Basic programming and mathematics. Freshers show projects or internships. Not a degree wall.

Direct fit
Computer Science · ECE / EEE · Mechatronics · Robotics & Automation · Instrumentation · Mechanical. Enter the programme directly.
Adjacent, with bridging
Physics, Mathematics, and other engineering disciplines with programming ability. Enter after a short bridge module.
Needs major bridging
Everyone else. Routed to foundation preparation first — not turned away, sequenced.
Why a soft gate, deliberately: a government programme is judged on employability and inclusion. A "direct-fit degrees only" filter would work against the very goals the scheme is funded to achieve — and would quietly exclude capable candidates. The assessment protects quality; the bands protect access.

Entry is mapped to national qualification levels so the certificate a student earns is recognised anywhere in India, not just inside BGR Neo.

09 · The training programme

Two tiers: a broad foundation everyone shares, then the specialisation only we can teach.

Foundation — everyone
~920 hours · about two-thirds
Industrial specialisation
~480 hours · one-third
Tier 1 · Simulation-first

The foundation

Mathematics, Python, basic machine learning, robotics fundamentals, and industrial safety context. Taught almost entirely in simulation and software — which is where the cost savings are.

  • Low capital cost: computers and free open-source simulators. No robots needed at this stage.
  • Maps to entry-level national qualifications for AI roles and automation basics.
Tier 2 · On real cells

The specialisation

Machine vision, manipulation, welding offline-programming, digital twins, cell integration, predictive maintenance and defect analytics — taught on real BGR Neo cells, against real shop-floor problems.

  • Higher national qualification levels (roughly 5 to 8), certified through the industrial automation council.
  • The capstone is a real integration problem — the student commissions something that works.

A graduate therefore leaves with breadth that any employer recognises and depth that only a live factory can produce.

14 · Roadmap

Win the Expression of Interest. Prove it on one cohort. Then scale.

Phase 0

Prepare

Assemble the EOI dossier: named technical experts, the investment plan, three years of audited financials.

Phase 1

Win the EOI

Submit to TNSDC and defend it before the selection committee. The wedge is the argument.

Phase 2

Pilot cohort

Foundation delivered in simulation; specialisation on one live cell. Measure placement, not attendance.

Phase 3

Scale

Add cells and cohorts. Turn on the college feeders — Naan Mudhalvan and training-partner routes.

Phase 4 · optional

Spin into a company

If the centre earns, structure it as its own entity — following the TNASDCL and semiconductor precedents.

Timing is an assumption, not a commitment. The pace is set by the TNSDC EOI calendar, which we do not control. Phases 0 and 1 should be treated as this financial year's work; everything after depends on winning.

Our assumption: the structure below. We have deliberately not estimated rupee figures — those must come from our own vendor quotes and finance, not from a slide.
